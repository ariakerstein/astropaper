# The Rogue Patient

_This is the first of a set of posts helping people navigate hard medical conditions in the (American) healthcare system._

## How did I get here?

**San Francisco, 2018.**

> Doctor: Ari, please sit down. We got your scan images back…It's not good news. Unfortunately these results are consistent with Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma. I know this isn't the news you were hoping to hear and I'm sure you have a lot of questions.
>
> Me: Stunned, the blood flushes from my head …I don't think I understand what you just said…uh, what does this mean? How bad is this?
>
> Doctor: An Oncologist will follow up shortly with more information. For now I'd probably avoid searching things on Google.

I searched Google, opening a pit in my stomach.

---

The plan: inject myself with poison to kill it faster than it can kill me. This is as counter-intuitive as it is rational and necessary. I look at the plastic tube in my forearm.

I suddenly feel gravity pull me into the plush leather chair. The irony of poisoning myself in comfort. It all hinges on this protocol, messy medical statistics and the promise of atomic interactions; a toddler corralling wild animals.

Should the plan fail, my personal cosmos dies: no more hugs, Saul's tender locks, Lev's joyous screeches, Lua embracing my leg as I limp up stairs like Capt. Ahab, Michelle's sweet voice calling 'Ari'. No more grand ideas, great food, smelling warm homes, whiteboard sessions with my team, crisp air on my cheek. Sunday walks in a garden disappear, along with electric purple flowers. No more hypnotically staring at golden dust diffusing in a ray of sunlight. Game over, and a wake of hardship and sorrow for my family and friends.

Ugh! I squeeze all of… _this.._ into my palm in vain attempts to strangle something. I look up, noting the machines on stands, IV drips, beeping things and sterile walls. My eyes drift to the floor. Black nurse clogs step into my teary peripheral vision, then back away tactfully. I clench my eyes and inhale, gagging on the smell of glue molecules. Why does that stretchy corrugated tape smell like that?

I lift my eyes to hers. _Let's do it_.

The Nurse hooks up the Rituximab to the orphaned end of the tubing. I pick up the hastily stapled sheet she printed out at my request. The second line reads - in 94 font - "SIDE EFFECTS CAN INCLUDE DEATH…". _Pointless,_ I think? I put it down. Let's try to relax.

## What I avoided by being an active patient:

- Avoided an unnecessary stem-cell transplant which would have included living away from my 3 small kids for months, and living in a bubble.
- Avoided intense follow-up chemotherapy treatments with low odds of success.
- Thought through potentially getting onto an experimental and dangerous (not to mention costly!) clinical trial.
- Altered a surgical procedure that would have had me immobilized for 6 weeks. Not to mention the damage my body would have absorbed.

How was I able to navigate an otherwise terminal cancer diagnosis and avoid unnecessary treatments and procedures recommended by some of the world's best oncologists?

And while this is based on my experience, this isn't only my story. These stories happen every day to people navigating the medical system. In fact, they are the normal default mode of engagement.

---

## Thesis

Navigating a hard medical condition is a war on multiple fronts, spanning biology to the healthcare system. **The key to achieving a good outcome is intelligent and active patient engagement.**

Said another way, a great way to achieve suboptimal medical outcomes is to listen to your doctor, unblinking and unquestioning.

We, regular people, go to the doctor for authoritative guidance about what to do. Rarely, however, do we ask ourselves what this doctor knows that would not be accessible through a knowledgeable community? Increasingly through AI tools (that can pass medical exams). Nor do we ask how current this person's knowledge is, or how they stay abreast of the latest innovations.

In short, we trust. And we trust because we have no real choice. This essay will equip patients (or caregivers) with a set of tools to emerge with the best possible outcome.

## Principles for Navigating Hard Medical Conditions

The aim of this essay is to outline a set of principles that will empower you - as a patient, or a loved one - to navigate the (American) healthcare system successfully.

I'll do this by outlining common ways in which people tend to engage the system, and why they tend not to work well. I'll then define a set of principles that will help you succeed.

### 1. Define your people/community, and delineate roles

The first thing I did when I got the call from my doctor that I was diagnosed was I sat down and wrote a list of names. This was not any list. I remember filtering my network for two specific attributes: high rationality, and the ability to separate that from emotional decision-making. I needed thought partners to help me understand my situation from first principles.

### 2. Medical decisions are one-way doors. Play out the scenarios

How much uncertainty is tolerable when making a life and death decision?

I really like what Jeff Bezos says in one of his annual letters about one-way versus two-way doors: 2-way doors ought to be made with a bias towards speed over perfection (you can always go back or iterate the decision). One-way doors in contrast are non-reversible and ought to be made with great care and deliberation.

The mental framework I used is to saturate my information gathering space such that I'm in the best possible place to make an informed decision under a hard constraint of time. Here's the algorithm:

1. Timebox the decision
2. Gather as much relevant information inside of the time window
3. Synthesize the learnings
4. Decide and gut-check (regret minimization)
5. Act

### 3. Consider alternatives, and especially the do-nothing scenario

It's important to do your own analysis. I had been pushing to start second-line treatment IMMEDIATELY. But if I think more holistically I can ask the question: what if I sit on my hands and do nothing for 3-6 months here?

Part of the motivation might be to see if yet more treatments come online as there's much in the current FDA pipeline. No medical professional would recommend it. Counter to intuition, sometimes waiting yields a better result.

### 4. A little bit of Chutzpah is helpful. You are the CEO of your care

Think of your care team as a set of consultants helping you - the CEO - make the best decision you can. Treat them in this way. Remember, they are here for you and not vice versa.
